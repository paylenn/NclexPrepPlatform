Preparing for Your NCLEX Exam

The National Council Licensure Examination (NCLEX) is a standardized exam that each state board of nursing uses to determine whether a candidate is prepared for entry-level nursing practice. Our resources are designed to help you navigate both the traditional NCLEX format and the new Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) introduced in April 2023.

Exam Preparation Timeline

1

2-3 Months Before Exam

  • Create a detailed study schedule
  • Begin content review of all nursing areas
  • Take a diagnostic practice test
  • Identify knowledge gaps
  • Practice 25-50 questions daily
2

1 Month Before Exam

  • Complete content review
  • Focus on weak areas
  • Take full-length practice exams weekly
  • Review all rationales thoroughly
  • Practice NGN question types
  • Increase to 75-100 questions daily
3

2 Weeks Before Exam

  • Focus on test-taking strategies
  • Review notes, flashcards, and mnemonics
  • Practice questions in timed settings
  • Review priority nursing interventions
  • Take a final comprehensive practice exam
4

Week of Exam

  • Light review of key concepts
  • Practice relaxation techniques
  • Prepare testing materials (ID, ATT)
  • Get adequate rest and nutrition
  • Visualize success
  • Limit studying day before exam

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Next Generation NCLEX (NGN)?

The Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) is an updated version of the NCLEX exam that was implemented starting April 1, 2023. It includes new question types designed to better assess clinical judgment skills. These new formats include extended multiple response, drag-and-drop, cloze (drop-down), highlight, and matrix/grid questions. NGN also includes case studies that present unfolding client scenarios with questions that follow the six steps of the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model.

How many questions are on the NCLEX?

The NCLEX-RN exam is a variable-length adaptive test. In the minimum-length exam, you'll answer 70 scored items plus 15 unscored pretest items, for a total of 85 questions. In the maximum-length exam, you'll answer 135 scored items plus 15 unscored pretest items, for a total of 150 questions. The exam will stop when the computer determines with 95% certainty whether you are above or below the passing standard, or when you reach the maximum number of questions.

How is the NGN scored?

NGN questions use several scoring methods:

  • +/- Scoring: Used when candidates may select unlimited options. Points are awarded for correct selections and deducted for incorrect selections.
  • 0/1 Scoring: Used when candidates must select a specific number of options. Points are awarded for correct selections, but no points are deducted for incorrect selections.
  • Rationale Scoring: Used for items that link a concept with its justification. Both parts must be correct to receive full credit.
All of these scoring methods allow for partial credit, unlike traditional NCLEX questions which are scored as either correct or incorrect.

How should I prepare for the clinical judgment questions?

To prepare for clinical judgment questions:

  • Understand the six steps of the NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model
  • Practice with case studies that require you to apply the nursing process
  • Focus on developing your ability to recognize relevant cues in client scenarios
  • Practice prioritizing care and interventions based on client needs
  • Review common clinical conditions and their typical presentations, complications, and nursing interventions
  • Work with all the new NGN question types to become familiar with their formats

How long should I study for the NCLEX?

The optimal study period varies by individual, but most successful candidates spend 1-3 months preparing for the NCLEX. It's generally recommended to study 1-2 hours per day, 5-6 days per week, for a total of about 8-12 hours weekly. What's the best way to review rationales for practice questions?

When reviewing rationales, follow these best practices:

  • Review rationales for BOTH correct and incorrect answers
  • Understand why the correct answer is right and why the distractors are wrong
  • Create a notebook for information from rationales that you didn't know
  • Look for patterns in the questions you miss and focus extra study time on those topics
  • Use rationales as a learning opportunity, not just to check if you were right
  • Consider how the question relates to the nursing process and clinical judgment
